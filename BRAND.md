# Volkman Farm Brand Foundation

A working document for design and copy decisions. If a choice on the site, on a label, or in a text message can't be justified against the positioning statement below, it shouldn't ship.

## 1. Brand Positioning Statement

Volkman Farm is a hand-delivered microgreens operation run by one homeschooling family of five out of a backyard shed in Sanford, Florida. It exists for Orlando-metro home cooks and neighborhood chefs who care that what they eat tonight was alive this morning, and who would rather text a neighbor than tap through a checkout. It stands against the seven-day distributor chain, the plastic clamshell, the subscription portal, and the implication that food has to come from somewhere bigger to be worth buying. The one sentence at the top of every decision: **we grow ten kinds of microgreens, cut them the morning we deliver them, and hand them to the people on our route.** If a design or copy choice doesn't make that easier to understand or more believable, it's the wrong choice.

## 2. Tagline

**Recommended primary, two lines:**

> Harvested the morning we deliver.
> On your counter by dinner.

This is what's on the homepage already. It does the entire job. It states the functional differentiator (day-of harvest) and the lived outcome (it's in your kitchen tonight) in fourteen words, with no adjective doing any heavy lifting. Keep it.

**Alternatives to keep in reserve:**

1. **Cut this morning. Yours tonight.** (Punchier, six words. Good for a sticker, a label, or social.)
2. **Grown in Sanford. Delivered by us.** (Leans on the local + hand-delivered angle. Good for the wholesale page.)
3. **A family. A shed. Ten greens. Your street.** (Story-shaped. Good for the About page hero, weaker as a universal tagline.)
4. **The microgreens that were still growing this morning.** (Single line, declarative. Works for an Open Graph image or an Instagram bio.)

## 3. Brand Personality

Five traits and two counter-traits.

- **Direct, never preachy.** Direct means we tell you the price, the route schedule, and what happens next, without hedging. Never preachy means we don't lecture about kale, sulforaphane studies, or the failures of industrial agriculture even when the case is sitting right there.
- **Neighborly, never folksy.** Neighborly means we write like a person who lives on your street and is asking if you want some greens. Never folksy means no "y'all," no "the missus," no straw hats, no aw-shucks routine. Sanford is a real place, not a costume.
- **Confident, never proud.** Confident means we know our greens are fresher than what's at Publix and we say so. Never proud means we don't talk about ourselves as artisans or craftspeople. We grow greens. They are good. That's the post.
- **Specific, never clinical.** Specific means we say "Friday morning, Lake Mary, sunrise," not "weekly deliveries available in select markets." Never clinical means no nutrition-label voice, no "rich in antioxidants and phytochemicals." We say "a handful covers your day's vitamin K" because that's a person talking.
- **Calm, never urgent.** Calm means standing weekly orders, pay-on-delivery, skip any week. Never urgent means no countdown timers, no "order in the next 2 hours," no scarcity theater. The actual scarcity (we cap routes) speaks for itself if we let it.

**Counter-traits (what we are NOT):**

- We are not a **lifestyle brand**. We don't sell aesthetic. We sell greens.
- We are not a **startup**. No "team," no "platform," no "mission statement" copy. A family runs this. The website looks like one person made it because one person did.

## 4. Voice and Writing Rules

### Word choices to embrace

street, neighborhood, route, morning, cut, harvest, tray, shed, family, kids, ten varieties, $3.50/oz, Venmo, Zelle, text, porch, dinner, Friday, Sunday, sunrise, handful, pile, scatter, top, fold in, on your counter, in your fridge, by dinner

### Word choices to ban

- **farm-to-table** (overused, means nothing)
- **artisan, artisanal, craft, crafted** (overclaiming for what we do)
- **curated, hand-curated** (we don't curate, we grow)
- **mission, vision, values** (in customer-facing copy; fine internally)
- **community** as a noun for our customers ("our community" is corporate-speak; "our neighbors" is honest)
- **sustainable, eco-friendly, green** (the second meaning of "green" is poisoned; also unverifiable)
- **superfood, nutrient-dense, nutrient-rich, functional food** (used by every powder hustler on Instagram)
- **journey, story, passion** (every food brand says these)
- **just**, **simply**, **literally** as filler ("just text us" becomes "text us")
- **leverage, utilize, optimize** (corporate)
- **um-dashes, em-dashes** of any flavor (use period, comma, colon, or parens)
- **clamshell** when describing our packaging (used negatively about competitors, fine; not as a positive product term for us)

### Sentence rules

- **Short sentences carry the page.** Five to twelve words. When a sentence runs long, break it.
- **Lead with the noun.** "Friday morning. We deliver to Lake Mary." not "Deliveries to Lake Mary happen on Friday mornings."
- **Periods over commas, commas over colons, colons over parens, parens over dashes.** No em-dashes, ever. Hyphens only inside compound words.
- **Numbers as numerals when they're informational.** $3.50/oz, 10%, ten varieties (the word is fine for "ten" because it's a brand quantity), 8 days from seed.
- **Lowercase "we," capital "Volkman Farm," capital city names.** Never "VolkmanFarm" or "VF" in copy.
- **One exclamation per page, max, and only if it's actually exclamatory.** "We'll text you when we're on the way" doesn't need one.

### When to say "we" vs. naming people

- Use **we** for everything customer-facing by default. "We deliver Fridays." "We text on the way." "We grow ten varieties."
- Name individuals only on the **About page** and in places where a specific human is doing a specific thing the reader can picture (e.g., "Albert built the shed," "the kids do the seeding"). Never name the kids by name in public copy. Refer to them as "the kids" or "our three kids."
- Never write **"the Volkman family"** as a third-person reference on our own site. That's how a newspaper writes about us, not how we write about us.

### How to talk about the kids

- They show up in the operation: seeding, packing trays, riding along on deliveries. Say that plainly when it's relevant.
- Do not make them the story. They are part of the story. The story is the greens.
- Do not write in the kids' voices. Do not invent quotes from them. Do not photograph them in marketing-shoot poses.
- No ages, no names, no school details. "Our three kids," "the kids," "our youngest helps pack."

### How to talk about freshness (without sounding clinical or making illegal claims)

- We can talk about **growing inputs**: organic non-GMO seeds, filtered water for growing, sanitized trays.
- We can talk about the **act of harvest**: cut, harvested, snipped, taken off the tray.
- We can talk about **time**: the morning of delivery, hours from tray to porch, what you eat tonight was alive this morning.
- We cannot talk about **post-harvest handling**: no washing, rinsing, drying, processing, cleaning. State regulations prohibit those activities, and our copy must never imply them. If it sounds like we did something to the greens after cutting, rewrite it.
- We cannot make **medical claims**. We can cite studied compounds (sulforaphane, vitamin K, betalains) without saying our greens cure, treat, prevent, or improve a condition.

### Before / after rewrites

**Before:** "At Volkman Farm, we are passionate about delivering the freshest, most nutrient-rich microgreens to our community. Our family-owned and operated farm utilizes sustainable growing practices to bring you a truly farm-to-table experience."

**After:** "We grow ten kinds of microgreens in a shed behind our house and hand them to you the morning we cut them. That's the whole business."

---

**Before:** "Our broccoli microgreens are a superfood powerhouse, packed with up to 40x the sulforaphane of mature broccoli, making them an essential addition to any health-conscious lifestyle."

**After:** "Broccoli microgreens carry up to 40x the sulforaphane of full-grown broccoli, ounce for ounce. The flavor is mild enough to hide in eggs or a smoothie. Most kids won't notice."

---

**Before:** "Subscribe to our weekly delivery service and unlock exclusive savings on your favorite varieties! Cancel anytime."

**After:** "Pick a standing weekly order and we'll knock 10% off. Skip any week, cancel any week. No card on file."

---

**Before:** "We are committed to sustainability and source only the highest-quality, organic, non-GMO seeds for our growing operation."

**After:** "We use organic non-GMO seeds and filtered water. We grow on sanitized trays in a converted shed."

---

**Before:** "Welcome to our family farm! Meet the Volkmans, a passionate homeschooling family on a mission to bring fresh, healthy food to the Orlando community."

**After:** "Five of us live in Sanford. Two parents, three kids, one shed full of trays. We started growing microgreens because we wanted better food on our own table, and the route grew from there."

## 5. Color Palette

The cream and ink foundation is doing real work and stays. The two accents need small adjustments for contrast and printability. Below is the full system with hex values, usage rules, and a designer-ready WCAG note.

### The core five

| Token | Hex | Name | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| `--cream` | `#fef6e9` | Paper cream | Page background. The single most identifying color in the brand. Never replaced. |
| `--cream-shadow` | `#f0e6d0` | Tray cream | Input fields, secondary surfaces, hover backgrounds for cards. Subtle, not loud. |
| `--ink` | `#221d14` | Ink | Body text, borders, primary logo color. Slightly deeper than current `#2b2418` for AAA contrast on cream. |
| `--sprout` | `#4f6b30` | Sprout green | Primary action color. Buttons, links on hover, active nav state, the underline on the logo. Slightly deeper than the current `#5c7a3a` for AA contrast on cream (4.5:1 minimum). |
| `--radish` | `#b04a30` | Radish red | Accent only. Used for the hover state on links, badges on standing orders, urgent admin notes, and the radish variety's variety-page accent. Never the primary CTA. |

### The supporting three

| Token | Hex | Name | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| `--beet` | `#8a2f5a` | Beet pink | Used sparingly, and only on variety-page art and produce illustration where the actual color of the food earns its place (beet stems, chard stems). Never used as a brand color in nav, buttons, or background. |
| `--chard` | `#d49a1f` | Chard yellow | Same rule. Variety illustration only. Yellow chard stems, mustard variety accent. |
| `--soil` | `#5a4a36` | Soil brown | The "warm neutral" between ink and cream. Use for muted text, captions, metadata (e.g., "Friday morning route"), footer copy. Replaces dropped-opacity ink for legibility. |

### What this palette is for

- **Retail (homepage, /microgreens/, variety pages):** cream + ink + sprout, with soil for metadata, radish as the hover accent, beet and chard only inside variety illustration.
- **Wholesale (/restaurants/):** the same palette, but tighter. Cream + ink + sprout + soil only. No beet, no chard, no radish in nav or buttons. Chefs read color as a signal of seriousness. Restraint reads as professional.

### When NOT to use a color

- **Never use sprout for body text.** It is a UI color, not a copy color. Body is always ink on cream.
- **Never use radish on a CTA button.** It is a hover and accent color, not a primary signal.
- **Never put cream on cream-shadow without a border.** Always ink-rule the boundary or the eye loses the form.
- **Never use beet, chard, or soil for a button.** They are illustration and metadata tones.

### Accessibility

- Ink on cream: ~14.5:1 (AAA)
- Sprout on cream: ~4.8:1 (AA for normal text, AAA for large)
- Radish on cream: ~5.1:1 (AA)
- Soil on cream: ~6.8:1 (AAA)

Designer should pin these in a Figma variable file so the next person doesn't drift.

### Why not the current `#5c7a3a` and `#c2563a`?

Current sprout sits at about 3.9:1 on cream, which fails AA for normal-sized body links. Bumping to `#4f6b30` keeps the same visual identity (a slightly grassy, slightly olive green) and gets us safely over. Current radish reads orange-ier than it should. `#b04a30` reads as the actual color of a daikon radish skin we'd grow, and prints more cleanly on a kraft-paper label, which matters because we'll be labeling clamshells by hand.

## 6. Typography Pairing

**Recommended:**

- **Display (h1, h2, h3, blockquote): Fraunces.** A modern serif with optical sizes and a warm, slightly idiosyncratic feel. Use SOFT and the WONK axis dialed back (around 0–25 of 100) so it reads warm without getting cartoonish. At display sizes, set tight (line-height 1.1, letter-spacing -0.01em).
- **Body: keep Courier-style mono, but switch from system Courier New to JetBrains Mono or iA Writer Mono S.** Courier New is fine on macOS, ugly on Android, inconsistent on Windows. JetBrains Mono is free, has real italics, has proper Unicode, and reads as "typewriter" without screaming "code editor."
- **System fallback stack for mono:** `'JetBrains Mono', 'Courier Prime', 'Courier New', Courier, monospace`.

**Why this pairing:**

The mono body is the brand's voice in typographic form. It looks handmade, it looks shipped-by-one-person, and it makes the cream paper feel like a printed page rather than a polished SaaS landing. Replacing it with a humanist sans would erase what makes the site instantly identifiable. The Fraunces display is the upgrade: it gives the headings personality and warmth without breaking the typewriter character of the page, the way a serif headline on a typed page in a real book does.

**Alternative if Fraunces doesn't sing:**

Source Serif 4 (a quieter, more traditional serif). Less personality, more invisibility. Pick it if the owner wants the page to feel calmer.

### Type scale

Mobile-first, fluid via `clamp()`. All sizes in rem.

| Token | Size | Line-height | Letter-spacing | Weight | Family |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| h1 | `clamp(2rem, 5.5vw, 3rem)` | 1.1 | -0.01em | 600 | Fraunces |
| h2 | `clamp(1.375rem, 3.75vw, 1.875rem)` | 1.2 | -0.005em | 600 | Fraunces |
| h3 | `clamp(1.125rem, 2.5vw, 1.375rem)` | 1.3 | 0 | 600 | Fraunces |
| body | `1.0625rem` (17px) | 1.65 | 0 | 400 | Mono stack |
| small | `0.875rem` (14px) | 1.5 | 0.01em | 400 | Mono stack |
| nav / button label | `1rem` | 1.4 | 0.08em | 700 | Mono stack |
| blockquote | `1.125rem` italic | 1.5 | 0 | 400 italic | Fraunces |

Mono body at 17px gives the visual density of a typewritten page without the eye-strain mono normally introduces at smaller sizes. The 1.65 line-height is intentionally loose to compensate for mono's fixed-width awkwardness on long lines.

### Self-hosting

Self-host both faces. No Google Fonts CDN. The site is a small static Eleventy build and a `woff2` file in `/assets/fonts/` is faster and more private than the CDN. Use `font-display: swap` and a fallback `size-adjust` so the cream page never flashes.

## 7. Photography and Imagery Direction

The owner has a phone, a window, and a shed. The photography direction has to be achievable that way, and it has to look like one family took the photos, because they did.

### What the photos should look like

- **Window light, single source, no flash.** Morning or late afternoon. Diffused by an overcast day or a sheer curtain. Soft shadows running one direction.
- **Top-down on a wooden cutting board, a linen cloth, or the cream paper itself.** Composition is centered and calm. A single tray of greens. A handful in a hand. A pile on a piece of toast. Negative space matters.
- **Color comes from the food, not the background.** The cream paper, the wooden board, the ink stems of beet and chard, the pink-red of radish. The set is monochrome. The greens are the color.
- **Hands in frame are fine, faces mostly not.** A hand cutting a tray. A hand passing a clamshell. A child's hand placing a seed. (No close-ups of kids' faces. Backs of heads, hands at work, a silhouette in the shed door is okay.)
- **The shed itself is photogenic. Use it.** Trays under grow lights at night. The fluorescent green glow inside a dark shed in the early morning. This is the most differentiated visual asset the brand owns. No one else has this exact shed.
- **Real plates, real kitchens.** When showing usage (e.g., "scatter on tacos"), shoot it in the actual kitchen with the actual taco. No food-styling toothpicks, no glycerin spray.

### What to avoid

- **No stock photos.** Of anything. Not even "placeholder." A blank cream rectangle is better.
- **No happy-family-in-matching-flannels poses.** This is the single biggest failure mode of a family brand. The family shows up in the work, not in the marketing shoot.
- **No chef in a white coat holding a sprig with tweezers.** Even on the wholesale page. Especially on the wholesale page.
- **No overhead-shot food blog plating** with seven garnishes, a wood board on a marble counter, a side of flaky sea salt, etc. We are not Bon Appétit.
- **No drone shots, no time-lapses, no fake "morning routine" Instagram reels.** Static is fine. Static is on-brand.
- **No image with a filter applied that wasn't already there.** If the photo needs Instagram's "Clarendon" to work, the photo doesn't work.
- **No watermarks on photos, ever.**

### Practical shot list for the next site update

1. Hero: top-down of a wooden board with five small piles of different microgreens. Cream background. One source of window light.
2. About: the shed exterior, daylight, the door open. No people in frame.
3. About: a hand placing seeds on a soaked paper towel in a tray. Just hands.
4. Variety pages: one photo per variety, three-quarters angle, single tray on cream, stems visible.
5. Delivery: the back of a car with a stacked crate of clamshells, parked on a residential street. Mailbox or porch in soft background. No people.

## 8. Iconography, Texture, and Motif

The site's identity is built on the cream paper and the typewriter body. The motif direction follows from there.

**Recommended direction: stamped, not drawn.**

- **One mark, used as a stamp.** A small monogram or initial — a `V` inside a square, or the tray-grid pattern as a 12x4 cell of dots — printed in sprout green or ink, the way a real letterpress mark would print on cream paper. Used once per page, never as decoration on every section.
- **Use the tray itself as a pattern.** The grow-tray grid (12 cells by 4 cells of soil divots) is the only "ornament" the brand needs. Use it as a quiet repeating background on the wholesale page hero, as a divider between sections, as the negative-space pattern on a label or business card.
- **Bullet markers as small filled squares.** Already used in the wholesale page (`list-style: square`). Keep it. Squares match the tray. Discs and dashes don't.
- **No line-art veggie icons.** No little drawings of a broccoli sprig or a carrot. Stock icon sets are the fastest way to make this brand look like every other Etsy farm.
- **No hand-drawn watercolors.** Wrong era and the wrong implied price point.
- **No script fonts, no flourishes, no curlicues.** The brand's hand is a typewriter, not a calligrapher.

**Texture:**

The cream is already the texture. Resist the urge to add paper grain, noise, or scan artifacts. If the screen reads too clean, the fix is a slightly off-white SVG noise overlay at 2-3% opacity, not a JPEG paper texture.

**Logo notes (for the next designer to react to):**

The current logo is a simple wordmark. That's the right call. If it needs evolution, the move is to set "Volkman Farm" in Fraunces small caps, with a single sprout-green square mark to the left, and nothing else. No leaves, no sun rays, no script "farm fresh" banner.

## 9. The Do-Not-Do List

1. **Never write "farm-to-table."** Overused. Means nothing. Use "we grow it and drive it to your house."
2. **Never put a stock photo of a chef on the wholesale page.** Our chefs are real people we already know. If we don't have a photo of one of them yet, leave the space empty.
3. **Never use a cursive or script font anywhere.** Wrong era, wrong brand, wrong implied price.
4. **Never use em-dashes.** Owner's call. Periods, commas, colons, parens. Hyphens only in compound words.
5. **Never use the word "community" to mean "our customers."** It's corporate fog. "Our neighbors" or "the families on our route" is honest.
6. **Never claim we wash, rinse, dry, or process the greens after harvest.** State regulations. Stick to growing inputs and the act of harvest.
7. **Never name or photograph the kids' faces in public copy.** The kids show up in the work, not in the marketing.
8. **Never add a countdown timer, a fake-scarcity banner, or a "10 people viewing now."** The real scarcity is that we cap routes. That's the story, and it stands on its own.
9. **Never replace the cream background.** Even on a single landing page. The cream is the brand.
10. **Never link to social media we don't actively post on.** A dead Instagram link is worse than no link. Owner posts when there's something to post or there's no Instagram link.
11. **Never use the phrase "passion project," "labor of love," or "our journey."** Every food brand says these. We don't.
12. **Never write a 600-word About page.** The story is short. Five people, one shed, ten greens, one route. Anything longer is filler.

## 10. Resolved Decisions

The owner reviewed the open questions and locked in the following. The UI Designer should build the system to honor these as constraints.

1. **Family name.** Implicit. "Volkman Farm" stays as the sign over the door. Personal warmth comes from "we", not from surnaming the kids.
2. **Homeschool angle.** Implicit. Mention once (currently in About). Do not turn it into a brand signal. The customer who notices is welcomed; the customer who doesn't is also welcomed.
3. **Wholesale visual identity.** Distinct. `/restaurants/` uses the tighter, more restrained treatment: cream, tray cream, ink, sprout, and soil only. No radish accent on the wholesale page. No variety-color illustration. More formal copy. Chefs read restraint as professional.
4. **Kids language.** Generic. "Our kids help pack" is the upper bound. Do not name kids, ages, or specific roles. No "our 9-year-old runs the seeding station" copy.
5. **Logo mark.** Yes. Design a small stamped square mark in addition to the existing wordmark. Use for favicon, social avatar, label-corner stamps, sticker designs, and any place the full wordmark is too wide. The wordmark remains primary; the mark is secondary.
