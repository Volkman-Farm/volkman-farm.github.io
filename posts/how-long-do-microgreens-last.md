We get this one more than any other question, usually at the door. The honest answer is about a week, sometimes ten days, and the range is that wide because it depends on the variety and almost entirely on what happens in your fridge.

Here is the part that matters more than the number. The clock on a green does not start when you buy it. It starts when it is cut. That is the whole reason we run the routes the way we do, and it is why a clamshell that lands on your porch Friday morning is not the same age as a tub off a shelf, even if they look identical. The store tub was cut somewhere else, packed, trucked, received, and set out. Days of the life you paid for are already spent. Ours are cut the morning they go out, so day one is actually day one. We wrote about the porch logic [here](/blog/why-we-deliver-to-porches/) if you want the longer version.

So, a week to ten days, starting the day it arrives. Sturdier varieties sit at the long end. [Pea](/greens/pea/) and sunflower have thick stems and hold up well. [Kale](/greens/kale/) and the other brassicas are reliable through the week. The delicate ones give up sooner: cilantro and the finer leaf varieties are happiest in the first four or five days, and [beet](/greens/beet/) is beautiful but not the one to leave for last. If you order a mix, eat down from the tender end.

## Cold and dry, in that order

Two things kill a clamshell early, and they are heat and water.

Heat is the easy one. Get them in the fridge when you bring the clamshell in. Not the counter, not the windowsill, not the car for another errand. The insulated bag holds them for the drive across the county, but that is a couple of hours of help, not a day of it.

Water is the one people get wrong, and the order matters. Our label says to wash before using, and that is the sequence we mean: wash the handful you are about to eat, right before it goes on the plate. What you do not want to do is wash the whole clamshell and then put it back in the fridge. Wet leaves sitting in a closed container turn slimy fast, and once a clamshell fogs up on the inside it is on a shorter clock. Store them dry, wash at the plate. If you open a clamshell and see condensation on the lid, tuck a folded paper towel in with the greens and it will pull the moisture off.

Store the clamshell in the main body of the fridge or the crisper, not jammed against the back wall where things freeze, and not in the door where the temperature swings every time somebody wants milk.

You will know when they are done. The stems go limp and a little slick, the color dulls, and there is a faint sour smell when you open the lid. Trust your nose. Greens that still stand up and smell green are fine.

One last thing worth saying plainly: a week is not a long time, and that is the tradeoff. We are not selling you something engineered to sit.
