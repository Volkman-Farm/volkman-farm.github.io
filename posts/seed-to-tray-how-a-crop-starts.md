Every tray you have read about here started on one morning, at one small table, with a scoop of soil and a handful of seed. We call it seeding day. It is the least dramatic part of the whole operation and the one that decides everything downstream. A crop that goes down wrong on day one does not fix itself later. So here is how a tray actually begins.

## A clean tray and good soil

We start with an empty tray and about six cups of Fox Farm Happy Frog, the same potting mix we mentioned in [the tour of the shed](/blog/the-shed-albert-built/). We buy it two cubic feet at a time. The soil goes in, then we level it with a flat hand or a spare tray pressed down on top. Level matters more than it looks. A high corner dries out fast, a low corner stays soupy, and the seed reads both as a reason to grow unevenly. We want a flat, even bed so the whole tray wakes up at the same time.

The tray itself is clean. We wash and dry equipment between crops, because a tray that carried the last round can carry its problems into the next one. That is the only thing we ever wash. The greens themselves are never touched with water after they are cut.

## How much seed goes down

Then the seed. It all comes from [True Leaf Market](https://www.trueleafmarket.com/), organic, and every variety has its own density. A tray of [radish](/greens/radish/) wants a different amount than a tray of tiny basil, and finding that number is most of the craft. Too little seed and the tray looks thin and grows lonely. Too much and the seedlings crowd, trap moisture against each other, and invite mold. We scatter by hand and by feel, aiming for a single even layer where seeds sit shoulder to shoulder without piling up.

Bigger seeds drink first. Our [peas](/greens/pea/) go for an overnight soak before they ever hit soil, so they swell and sprout in a hurry instead of sitting there deciding. Smaller seed goes down dry, straight onto the leveled bed.

## Water, the filtered kind

Once the seed is spread we water it in. This is where the filtered water earns its place. Ours is pH balanced to 6.0, which is what these tender young roots prefer, and it is free of the chlorine that comes out of a garden hose. On seeding day we water from the top with a gentle hand so we do not wash the seed into drifts and bare patches. After this first drink, most trays get their water from the bottom for the rest of the run, so the surface stays drier and calmer while the roots reach down for it.

Small inputs, done the same way every morning. Six cups of soil, the right weight of seed, a careful first watering. None of it is exotic. The discipline is in repeating it.

## Into the dark

Then the tray leaves the table and goes somewhere surprising. Not under the lights. Into the dark, stacked and covered, for a few days while the seed germinates and pushes roots down before it ever sees a lamp. That blackout stretch is its own subject, and we will walk through it in a growing note soon.

For now the crop is started. A flat bed, an even scatter of organic seed, one clean drink of filtered water, and a couple of weeks of patience between that morning and your porch.
