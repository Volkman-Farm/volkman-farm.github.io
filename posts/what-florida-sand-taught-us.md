We closed on this place and the first thing we wanted to do was dig. Everybody does. You walk the lot, you find the spot that looks right from the kitchen window, and your hands start itching for a shovel. We have written before about how we made ourselves [wait and watch instead](/blog/mapping-the-yard-before-touching-it/), and that was the right call. But we did dig once, early, just to see what we were standing on. That is the week one story, and it is short, because the ground told us everything in about ten minutes.

The shovel went in easy. Too easy. No resistance, no clods, no dark crumb turning over. What came up was pale and loose and ran off the blade like it wanted to be somewhere else. Sand. Not sandy soil, not soil with some sand in it. Sand, the kind you would not be surprised to dig up at the beach, because a long time ago that is more or less what this was.

Then we did the thing you should do before you trust any ground. We poured a bucket of water into the hole and watched. It did not pool. It did not even hesitate. It was gone, straight down, like we had poured it into a colander.

That is the whole problem in one picture. Water that leaves that fast takes everything with it. You can feed sand like this all the fertilizer you want and most of it is past the roots and gone before the plant gets a word in. The dark spongy stuff a plant actually wants to grow in, the part that holds water and holds food in place, is barely here. Under one percent for ground like ours, and what little there is burns off quick in the heat. A tomato dropped into that on day one does not stand a chance, and we would have blamed ourselves, or the seedling, or the weather, and never the thing that was actually wrong.

Because here is what nobody tells you when you move to Florida with a garden already planted in your head. "Just plant a garden" is advice written for somewhere else. It assumes soil. It assumes the ground is a bank you can make a withdrawal from. Our ground is not a bank. It is closer to an empty lot with very good drainage. The dirt is not the starting point here. The dirt is the project.

So we did not plant. We [piled up mulch and waited a year](/blog/permaculture-without-the-jargon/) instead, which sounds like doing nothing and is actually the whole fix, just stretched over twelve months instead of one weekend. Sand does not turn into soil because you are frustrated with it. It turns into soil because you keep covering it and feeding it and letting the slow things underground do the slow part. We are still not all the way there. You do not fix a soil in a season, you build one over years.

Week one taught us to stop arguing with the ground and start building it. Just about everything we grow in the yard now traces back to that one afternoon with a shovel and a bucket of vanishing water.

The greens never touch the sand, of course. They grow in trays out in the shed, in clean media and filtered water, which is a story for another day.
