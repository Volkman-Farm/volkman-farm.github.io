Tuesday is taco night at our house. Every week, all year, nobody votes on it. Something goes in a pan, tortillas get warmed, a lime gets cut into wedges, and dinner is basically done. It is the one meal we never have to think about, which is exactly why it is worth thinking about a little.

The part that usually goes missing is crunch. Shredded romaine is what we reach for, and romaine is honest work. It holds its snap, it does not go limp the second it touches a warm tortilla, and it is a long way better than iceberg. What it does not do is taste like anything. You get the texture and nothing else, and on a plate with cumin and lime and charred onion, that is a wasted slot.

Two of our greens fill that slot and bring flavor with them.

## Radish where the lettuce used to go

[Radish](/greens/radish/) micros are the peppery ones. The stems snap, and the heat arrives a second after the crunch does, closer to horseradish than to black pepper. Against a fatty filling, that bite does the same work a pickled onion does. It cuts through and resets your mouth for the next one.

You can keep a little romaine underneath if you want the volume. The radish is doing a different job than the lettuce was, so the two are not really competing.

Use more than you think. A pinch is a garnish, and a garnish is not what a taco needs. We put down a real layer, enough that every bite gets some. If you are cooking for people who flinch at heat, go lighter and let the sour cream do the rest, because radish gets sharper as the pile gets deeper.

## Cilantro without the cutting board

The street taco formula is onion and cilantro and nothing else, and it has been right for a long time. [Cilantro](/greens/cilantro/) micros slot straight into that spot. They are not a watered down version of the herb, so do not go in expecting subtle. The flavor is strong and green and citrusy, more herb and less soap, and the stems are tender enough that there is nothing to chop and no woody bits to pick out.

Here is the part worth saying out loud. Albert was one of the soap people. Cilantro from the store tasted like dish rag to him for years, and he picked it off things, and taco night at our house ran without it. Then we started growing it, and the micro version did not do that at all. It tasted like an actual herb instead, strong and bright, and somewhere in there he stopped picking it off and started asking for it. He is a cilantro guy now, which nobody who knew him ten years ago would believe.

We will not promise that happens for everyone, because plenty of people taste soap in the herb and always will. But if you wrote cilantro off after a bad bunch from the grocery store, the young version is a genuinely different experience, and it is worth one honest try.

## Put the bowl on the table

Here is the only technique that matters, and it is the same one we gave for [eggs](/blog/microgreens-on-eggs-three-ways/). The greens go on last, and they go on raw. Steam off a hot tortilla will wilt them flat in about a minute, so building the tacos in the kitchen and carrying the plates out is how you lose the crunch you paid for.

So we set the greens out in a bowl with the salsa and the lime and let everyone top their own at the table. It also means the kids can see exactly what is going on their food, which turns out to matter more than any argument we could make about it.

One handling note. Keep the clamshell closed and cold until dinner, since dry and cold is what keeps them crisp, and we said more about that in the [shelf life post](/blog/how-long-do-microgreens-last/). Our label says wash before using, so wash the handful you are about to put out, right before it goes in the bowl.
