Most small farms that sell straight to you make you come to them. A folding table at the Saturday market. A pickup lot where you grab a box between four and six, on their clock, not yours. It works, and plenty of good farms run that way. We just decided to flip it. We bring the greens to your porch, cut that same day, and you do not have to be home when we do.

## The freshness clock starts at harvest

A microgreen is at its best the day it leaves the tray. Every hour after that is a small step down. When you drive to a market stall, those greens were usually cut the day before and have been sitting in a cooler since. When we set a clamshell on a neighbor's step, the clock started that morning in the [shed](/blog/the-shed-albert-built/) a few miles away. Short clock, better green. The whole point of buying local is the distance, and a pickup lot quietly adds a day back onto it.

## One route a day

We run three routes, and each neighborhood has its day.

Our Friday morning route covers Seminole County: [Lake Mary](/delivery/lake-mary/), [Sanford](/delivery/sanford/), [Winter Springs](/delivery/winter-springs/), and [Oviedo](/delivery/oviedo/). Sanford is home base, so the trays come out at sunrise a few blocks from where they grew, and the first porches have them within the hour.

Our north Orlando route runs Monday and Wednesday evenings: [Casselberry](/delivery/casselberry/), [Maitland](/delivery/maitland/), [Winter Park](/delivery/winter-park/), and [College Park](/delivery/college-park/). Those are the evenings we are already headed that way to take the kids to taekwondo and rock climbing, so the route rides along with the family calendar. We cut in the afternoon and drop by dinner, which also keeps us off your porch during the workday.

[Downtown Orlando](/delivery/downtown-orlando/) gets its own Sunday afternoon route, because our church is in Parramore and we are already downtown that day. Greens cut Sunday morning, in your hands before dinner, ready for the week ahead.

That is nine neighborhoods, three routes, and one simple rule: one route a day means nothing rides around in a hot car waiting for the next stop across town.

## Why a porch and not a parking lot

A porch drop asks nothing of you. You do not have to leave work early, you do not have to stand in a lot, and you do not have to be waiting at the door. We text the morning of delivery with an ETA. Everything rides in an insulated cooler bag from the shed to your block, so it is still cold when we set the clamshell on your step, and a sealed 64 oz clamshell keeps just fine in the fridge until you get to it. Microgreens take almost no fridge space and hold for the week, so a Friday drop is still good the following Thursday.

It is also just friendlier. We would rather hand greens to a neighbor, or set them where a neighbor will find them, than run a counter. Over a season you learn a street. You learn who cooks, who has kids sneaking pea shoots off the cutting board, who wants the same order every week without being asked.

## Tell us your street

If your neighborhood is on one of the three routes, you are already covered. If you are close but not listed, tell us anyway. We add stops to an existing route without much trouble, and that is usually how a new street gets its day.

The greens are cut the day we bring them and grown a few miles from your door. [Start an order](/order/) and we will see you on the route.
