Every bag of greens we hand to a neighbor starts in one small building out back. People picture a greenhouse. It is not that. It is a shed, brand new, from [Superior Sheds](https://www.superiorsheds.com/locations/superior-sheds-retail-longwood/) in Longwood, and Albert spent weeks turning an empty box into a working farm.

## It starts with the pad

Before the shed could go up, it needed something solid and dry to sit on. Our neighbor (and wonderful friend) Tim poured the concrete pad for it, and we built it up a few inches off the grade on purpose. The water table sits high here in Central Florida, and a slab poured flush with the yard is a slab that stays damp. Raising it keeps the floor dry, which keeps the room dry, which keeps the crops happy. It was a good neighbor's help, and the whole farm sits on it.

## From empty box to grow room

When the shed itself arrived it was four walls and a roof. Everything else we added ourselves. Albert insulated it, ran the wiring, brought in plumbing, and hung the drywall, so the inside is a clean, sealed room instead of a hot storage shed. That part matters more than it sounds. Microgreens are fussy about temperature and moisture, and a bare Florida shed in July is neither cool nor calm.

## The racks

Inside are two racks right now. Each rack has five levels, and each level holds up to twenty trays. That is the working farm: rows of shallow trays, each one a [different crop](/greens/) at a different age.

We built for more than we use today. The room is wired and planned to hold six racks when we need them. Starting with two keeps us honest. We would rather run two racks well than six racks poorly, and grow into the space as the delivery routes fill in.

## Light around the clock

Every level carries two full-spectrum LED fixtures, so a full rack runs ten lights top to bottom. Whenever a level has trays on it, those lights stay on twenty-four hours a day. Microgreens are harvested young, in a sprint of about two weeks, and steady light through that sprint gives us even, sturdy trays instead of pale, leggy ones reaching for a window.

## Keeping a Florida summer outside

The hardest part of growing here is not the growing. It is the weather. So the shed has its own climate. A small LG air conditioner holds the temperature down, and a Midea Cube dehumidifier pulls the damp out of the air. Together they keep the room steady while it is ninety-five and soupy outside. The lights run constant, and the air conditioner and dehumidifier do the heavy lifting on hot afternoons. We are still measuring what that costs us each month, and when we have a real number we will put it right here rather than guess at it.

## What goes in the trays

The growing part is simple on purpose. Each tray gets about six cups of Fox Farm Happy Frog soil, a potting mix we buy two cubic feet at a time. Seed comes from True Leaf Market, all organic. The water is 6.0 pH balanced, exactly what the plants prefer. Small inputs, repeated with care, every single day.

That is the whole machine. A sealed room, two racks, twenty lights, a bag of soil, a seed order, and clean water. Nothing exotic. The work is in doing the same small things right, tray after tray, morning after morning.
