When we walked through [seeding a tray](/blog/seed-to-tray-how-a-crop-starts/), we left it in a strange place. The fresh tray, watered and seeded and ready, does not go under the lights. It goes into the dark. We said the blackout stretch was its own subject, so here it is. It is the quietest few days of a crop's life and the ones that decide how the whole thing turns out.

The setup is not fancy. Once a tray is seeded and watered in, we stack another tray on top of it and set a brick on that. Then it goes into a dark corner of the shed and sits. No lights, no fussing, no lifting the lid to check every hour. For most of our varieties that is about three days. A few of the slower seeds want longer, up to ten. It runs per variety, and part of getting to know a new seed is learning how long it likes to sit in the dark before it is ready to come up.

## Why the dark, and why a brick

Two things are going on under that weight.

The dark is for the seed. A seed does not need light to germinate. It needs moisture and steady contact with the soil, and darkness gives it both by keeping the surface from drying and by letting the seed spend its energy sending a root down instead of reaching a leaf up. Start a tray under bright light and you get a thin, hesitant stand. Start it in the dark and the roots go first, which is the order you want.

The brick is for the stems. As the seedlings wake up and start to push, the weight pushes back. That little argument between the crop and the brick is what builds a thick, even mat of strong stems instead of a sparse and floppy one. The bigger seeds we soak overnight, like the [peas](/greens/pea/), come up with real force behind them and will heave a brick and a tray clean off the stack. The first time you find a tray that has lifted its own weight in the night, you understand what all that pushing is for.

## Knowing when it is done

The rule for coming out of the dark is simple: the tray is done when the seeds have sprouted. Not when a timer goes off, not when it is convenient. We lift the top tray, and if the surface has turned into a carpet of sprouts reaching upward, blackout is over and the tray graduates to the light.

Those shoots come out pale. Yellow, almost white, because they have never seen a lamp, and that is exactly how they should look at this stage. Under the [shed lights](/blog/the-shed-albert-built/) they green up fast, often inside a day, as the leaves catch on to their real job. A tray of pale [kale](/greens/kale/) that looked half-dead in the dark turns deep green almost while you watch.

That is the blackout. A stack of trays and a brick in a dark corner, doing the least glamorous work in the whole operation and most of the important work too. Get the dark right and the weight right, and the rest of the grow mostly takes care of itself.
