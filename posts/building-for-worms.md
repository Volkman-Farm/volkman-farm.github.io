If you want to know what a patch of ground is really made of, watch what chooses to live in it. Ours came with ants.

Not a few. Whole cities of them, cones of pushed-up sand scattered across the bare spots, busiest in the hottest, driest, most sun-blasted corners of the lot. For a while we read that as a nuisance. Then we started reading it as a report. The ants were not the problem. They were telling us what kind of ground this was, and they were right.

Ants love bare Florida sand. It is loose, it drains fast, it bakes in the sun, and it stays that way. That is a feature if you are an ant digging tunnels. The trouble is that the thing we want most in a yard wants the exact opposite.

## What worms are asking for

Worms are the animal you want to see when you turn over a shovelful of ground, because worms only show up where the soil is already doing its job. They need it cool. They need it moist. They need something to eat, which means dead leaves and roots and the slow rot that sand simply does not offer. Bare sand under a July sun is none of those things. It is hot, it is dry, and there is nothing down there for a worm to make a living on. So they leave, or they never come, and the ants keep the place to themselves.

We [wrote last week](/blog/what-florida-sand-taught-us/) about pouring a bucket of water into a hole and watching it vanish. That same drainage is why the sand runs hot and empty. Organic matter, the dark spongy stuff that holds water and holds food, sits under one percent in ground like ours, and what little there is burns off fast in the heat. And sand does not hang onto nutrients even when you add them. It has almost no grip. Feed it and the next rain rinses most of it straight past the roots and gone, the same way that bucket disappeared. Hot, hungry, and leaky. Perfect for ants. Impossible for worms.

## Mulch is the lever

Here is the part that took us a while to trust, because it looks like doing nothing.

You do not fight the ants and you do not truck in a new yard. You put down mulch. Three to four inches of it, a real blanket, not a decorative sprinkle. And then that layer quietly does about four jobs at once.

It shades the ground, so the surface underneath can run roughly twenty degrees cooler on a summer afternoon than the bare sand baking next to it. It slows the water down and holds moisture instead of letting it flash off. It feeds the decomposers, the fungi and the bugs and eventually the worms, because now there is dead plant matter for them to eat. And as all of that breaks down, it starts building the very organic matter the sand never had, the stuff that finally gives nutrients something to cling to.

Cool, damp, and full of food. That is worm country. You are not planting worms. You are building the neighborhood and letting them move in on their own.

This is the same slow trick behind the [mulch mounds](/blog/permaculture-without-the-jargon/) we piled up and left alone for a year before anything went in them. Give the ground what it is missing, wait, and the biology handles the rest. It is not fast. Under a blanket of mulch the balance tips one season at a time, sand toward soil, ants toward worms. But it tips in the right direction, and it keeps tipping as long as you keep it covered.
