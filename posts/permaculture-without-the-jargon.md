Permaculture has a branding problem. Say the word out loud and people picture a certificate course, a whiteboard covered in zone diagrams, and someone explaining swales at you for an hour. All of that exists. None of it is the point.

The point fits in one sentence: work with the yard you have.

Watch what your yard already does. Put things where they already want to be. Let one system feed the next. Waste nothing you can use twice. Every technique in every thick permaculture book is in service of that sentence, and you do not need the vocabulary to start.

## Design, not decoration

Permaculture is not a garden style. There is no permaculture "look." It is the decision-making that happens before anything goes in the ground: where the water goes after a storm, where the shade falls in August, what feeds what. A yard designed this way might look wild or might look tidy. What makes it permaculture is that the pieces connect.

We are a family with a microgreens shed, three kids, a flock of chickens, and a yard in its first years. We would never call ourselves experts. But the pieces are starting to connect, and that part costs nothing.

## The loops we already run

Both of ours go through the chicken run.

When we harvest a tray of [microgreens](/greens/), what is left behind (spent soil, roots, the stems that did not make the bag) goes straight to the chickens. They eat what they want and scratch through the rest. The trash can never sees it.

The second loop is stranger and better. Scraps the chickens cannot eat go into a compost bin inside the run. Black soldier flies find the bin and lay their eggs there, and the larvae do the composting for us, eating through the scraps. When they are ready to pupate, they climb out of the bin on their own. The chickens are waiting. Nobody harvests anything. The larvae deliver themselves, and the birds turn kitchen waste into protein.

[The shed](/blog/the-shed-albert-built/) sits on the same kind of thinking. The water table is high here, so instead of fighting wet ground we raised the pad above it. Work with the yard you have, including the parts of it you cannot see.

## A year of mulch, then the first plantings

We did not plant into bare Florida sand, because bare Florida sand eats garden plans for breakfast. Instead we piled mulch and let it sit. For a year those piles did the quiet work: breaking down, holding water, turning themselves into soil the sand never offered.

Into those mounds we have planted the first residents of what we hope becomes a food forest: a lemon tree, two mango trees, two blueberry bushes, a banana, a pineapple, sweet potatoes, Seminole pumpkin, okra, butterfly peas, sunflowers, basil, and shampoo ginger. Some of that list is for the table, some is for the soil, some is for the pollinators, and some is there to teach us what this yard will actually grow.

The food forest also has a manager, and it is neither of the parents. Our oldest daughter runs it: what goes in, where it goes, what needs water and what needs leaving alone. The kids do more than help around here. Parts of this yard are theirs.

The patience was the point. A garden placed by guesswork gets moved. A mound built for a year gets to keep its tree.

## Written for here, not somewhere else

Most gardening advice assumes a place with seasons and soil. Central Florida has neither in the usual sense. Our ground is sand. Our summer is the hard season, not winter. Our real planting time arrives in fall, when the rest of the country is shutting the garden down.

So the permaculture we practice has a Florida accent. Mulch first, plant second. Summer is for cover, compost, and patience. Sand is not a problem to fix in one weekend; it is a soil to build over years, one layer at a time.

## The honest list

What is running right now: the chicken loops, the mulch mounds and their first plantings, and soil that gets a little darker every season. What is still ahead, and will show up here as each one becomes real: catching rain where it falls, and summer cover crops for the beds we have not built yet.

That is permaculture without the seminar. No certificate required, no jargon, one sentence doing all the work.
