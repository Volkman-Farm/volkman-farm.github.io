Eggs are the easiest place to start putting microgreens to work. You already make them a few mornings a week, they take to a handful of greens without complaint, and the pairing asks you to learn nothing new. Here is how we do it three ways at our house, and which of our ten varieties earns a spot in each.

One rule runs through all three: greens go in late, or they go on raw. The tender ones wilt to nothing if you cook them, and the flavor that made you buy them cooks off with the color. We said as much about [pea tendrils](/blog/pea-tendrils-the-gateway-green/) last week. Heat is not their friend, and eggs do not change that.

## Scrambled: fold in at the very end

Soft scrambled eggs are the gentlest of the three, but they still cook fast and hot. So we pull the pan off the burner while the eggs are just short of done, then fold a small handful of greens through the leftover warmth. Ten seconds, no more. They soften a touch, hold their color, and season the eggs from the inside instead of sitting on top as a garnish.

[Broccoli](/greens/broccoli/) is the pick here. It is mild enough that a kid who would riot over a green fleck eats it without a word, and it is the variety we reach for when we want the nutrition without the argument. If you want something with more to say, [arugula](/greens/arugula/) folds in the same way and brings a peppery edge that cuts the richness of the eggs.

## Omelet: greens in the fold

An omelet gives you a pocket, and a pocket is a good place for greens that would rather not touch the pan at all. Cook the omelet the way you always would. Just before the fold, lay a line of micros down the center with the cheese. The egg closes over them, they warm through in the steam, and they never meet direct heat.

This is where the sturdier greens shine. [Kale](/greens/kale/) micros keep their shape in the fold and taste like a gentler version of the grown leaf. [Cilantro](/greens/cilantro/) turns a plain omelet into something closer to a Mexican breakfast, especially with a spoon of salsa alongside.

## Fried, sunny side up: raw on the yolk

The last way is the simplest and, honestly, our favorite. Fry an egg however you like it, keep the yolk runny, and pile the greens on top raw. No cooking at all. The hot white and the broken yolk do all the warming they need, and you get the full crunch and flavor of the green with nothing lost to the pan.

This is the method for the delicate ones. Pea tendrils go here, sweet and crisp against the yolk. So does anything with a bite you want to keep sharp: a pinch of [radish](/greens/radish/) or mustard on a fried egg wakes the whole plate up. Toast underneath, yolk broken over it, greens on top. Hard to do better before the coffee is even poured.

So the whole method is one idea. Cook the eggs, then add the greens. Scrambled, fold them in off the heat. Omelet, tuck them in the fold. Fried, pile them on raw. The more delicate the green, the later it goes in, and the less it should ever meet the pan.
