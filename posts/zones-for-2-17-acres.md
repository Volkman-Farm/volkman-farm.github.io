The permaculture books all draw the same picture. A house in the middle and five rings around it. Zone 1 is the herbs by the kitchen door, zone 2 is the chickens and the orchard, zone 3 is the crop field, zone 4 is pasture and firewood, zone 5 is wild land you never touch. It is a good picture. It also quietly assumes a full farmstead underneath it, with pasture to rotate and a woodlot to cut.

We have 2.17 acres in Sanford. The decimals are the county's, not ours. They also zoned it A-1, agricultural, which means the land technically had a zone before we ever drew one. It is an in-between size: several times more ground than a subdivision yard, nowhere near the acreage the book diagram wants. There is no pasture here and no woodlot, and at our size there never will be. Draw the five rings at book scale and zone 4 runs off the property line.

For a while that made us think the zone chapter only half-applied to us. Then it clicked, and the click is the whole post: zones were never about distance. They are about attention. Zone 1 is not "near the house." It is "the stuff you tend every day," and it sits near the house because walking gets expensive when you do it twenty times a day. The rings are a way of saying: put things where your feet already go, and match each thing's neediness to how often you naturally pass it. On forty acres you measure that in walking. On ours you measure it mostly in habit, though two acres is enough that the far end is a real walk, and we notice it every time it rains.

## Our rings, honestly drawn

Zone 0 is the kitchen. That part survives at any scale. It is where the greens land, where the scrap container fills, where the day gets decided.

Zone 1 is the shed, and this is where our map breaks the textbook. By the book, a production crop belongs way out in zone 3. But nothing on this property gets more attention than [that little building](/blog/the-shed-albert-built/). It gets visited morning and evening at minimum: seeding, watering, checking trays, harvest. The market garden does not live three rings out here. It lives closer than the herbs would, because it is the thing that cannot miss a day.

The chicken run shares that inner ring, more or less. The birds get food and water daily, and the trip does double duty, since the spent trays from the shed walk out with us and go straight over the fence to the flock. One walk, two chores. That is the zone idea working exactly as [the books](/blog/permaculture-without-the-jargon/) intend, just compressed.

The food forest mounds sit a ring further out, in neediness as much as in steps. Mulched trees mostly want to be left alone, checked on, watered through a dry stretch, and otherwise trusted. Though it is worth saying the rings are drawn per person. Our oldest daughter manages the mounds, so on her map they sit closer in than they do on ours. This property has five Volkmans on it, and it does not have one zone map. It has five.

And zone 5. The books say every design should keep a wild place you do not manage at all, where you go to learn instead of work. This is where the acreage finally earns its keep. As we said in [the mapping post](/blog/mapping-the-yard-before-touching-it/), most of this land is still unbuilt, and we have stopped feeling behind about that and started calling it what it is: our zone 5, for now. It is the ground we watch instead of touch. When a piece of it finally teaches us enough, it graduates inward and becomes a bed or a mound, and the wild edge redraws itself. Some of it we expect to stay wild for good, and that is a feature of the design, not a leftover.

So that is our version. Five rings of attention, not five rings of distance, drawn on 2.17 acres and redrawn often. The book diagram survives the rescaling fine, as long as you keep the question that was always underneath it: how often will my feet actually pass this?
