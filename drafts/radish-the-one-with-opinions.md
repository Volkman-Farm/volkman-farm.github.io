Last week we called [pea tendrils the gateway green](/blog/pea-tendrils-the-gateway-green/), the one we hand somebody who is not sure about any of this. Radish is the opposite. Radish does not care whether you were ready.

Bite one and you get the peppery snap of a whole radish, clean and sharp and gone in a second. It is brighter than mustard and a good deal sharper than arugula. We grow a daikon variety because the heat comes through cleanest that way, without the muddy bitterness some radish seed brings. It is the most opinionated tray on our shelves, and people tend to sort themselves quickly into liking it a lot or not at all.

## The fastest thing in the shed

Radish is also our quickest crop, six to eight days from seed to cut. Nothing else we grow turns around that fast. It comes barreling out of [the blackout stack](/blog/the-blackout-phase/) while the slower seed is still deciding, and it greens up under the lights almost the same day.

We cut radish at the cotyledon stage, while the crop is still those first two rounded seed leaves and before any true leaves show. That is on purpose. Let radish run past that point and the flavor turns coarse and the stems get stringy. Cut at the right hour and you get the whole pepper hit with none of the toughness. Fast crops are less forgiving that way. There is no window to be casual about.

## Where the heat belongs

Radish is not a salad you eat alone by the bowl. It is a tool. Put it anywhere something rich needs cutting through, and it does the job that a squeeze of lime or a pickled onion does.

Fatty fish, for one. A pile on smoked salmon and cream cheese is close to perfect. Same with a banh mi, where it sits right in with the pickled vegetables like it was always part of the recipe. It belongs on avocado toast, which is the pairing everybody arrives at eventually. And it goes on top of a bowl of ramen or pho at the very last second, after the bowl leaves the stove, so it wilts a little and keeps its bite.

Tacos too, though we have enough to say about that one to give it its own post shortly.

Nutritionally radish carries a real load: high in vitamin C, vitamin B6, and folate, and it brings sulforaphane, the same compound that gets broccoli micros all their attention. We are not going to make a health claim out of it. It is a green with a strong personality that happens to be good for you.

If you have been ordering the mild end of our list and want to find out what the other end tastes like, [add radish to your next order](/greens/radish/). Start with a small amount on something rich. You will know within one bite which side you land on.
