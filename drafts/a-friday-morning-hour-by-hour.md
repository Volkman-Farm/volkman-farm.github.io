A Friday runs on a clock we did not pick. The greens set it. A microgreen is at its best the hour it leaves the tray, so the whole morning is arranged around keeping that hour short: cut late, pack fast, drive once. We wrote a while back about [why we deliver to porches](/blog/why-we-deliver-to-porches/) instead of running a pickup lot. This is what one of those Fridays actually looks like from the inside, start to finish.

**Before five.** The alarm goes off in the dark. Coffee first, then a headlamp and out across the yard to the [shed](/blog/the-shed-albert-built/) while the rest of the street is still asleep. We already know the week's orders down to the ounce, so there is no deciding to do, only cutting. The order sheet is taped to the shelf where we can see it.

**Sunrise.** Harvest. This is the part that cannot be rushed and cannot be moved. Every tray on the Friday list comes off the rack, and we cut straight into the 64 oz clamshells, weighing as we go. Nothing gets a rinse. The greens are never touched with water after the knife, so what goes in the clamshell is exactly what grew: cut, weighed, closed. A tray of [kale](/greens/kale/) here, a mix there, cilantro for the houses that order it every single week. By the time the sun is fully up, the shelf is emptier and the cooler bag is filling.

**Half past six.** The packing line, which is a folding table and whatever kids are awake. Five Volkmans live here and most of them end up with a job on a Friday. One seals lids, one checks labels against the sheet, one lines the finished clamshells into the insulated bag with the cold packs. It is not a factory. It is a kitchen table with a purpose, and it moves faster than you would think once everyone knows their piece.

**Seven.** The texts go out. Every neighbor on the morning's route gets a note with a rough ETA, so nobody has to wait by the door and nobody is surprised by a bag on the step. Then the bag goes in the car, cold packs and all, and we pull out of the driveway with the whole route sitting in one insulated cooler behind the seat.

**The drive.** We run it nearest to farthest so nothing rides around warm any longer than it has to. [Sanford](/delivery/sanford/) comes first, because home base is a few blocks from the first porches and those greens are the freshest they will ever be. Then over to [Lake Mary](/delivery/lake-mary/), then east across the county to [Winter Springs](/delivery/winter-springs/), and out to [Oviedo](/delivery/oviedo/) at the far corner. One loop, one direction, no backtracking across town in a hot car.

At each stop the clamshell comes out of the cooler bag and goes on the porch. The bag stays with us. That is the trick to a porch drop in a Florida July: the cold travels in the insulated bag, and the sealed clamshell holds its own for the short walk from car to doormat and then for the week in your fridge after. You do not have to be home. You just have to have a porch.

**Mid-morning.** The last Oviedo clamshell is on its step and the cooler bag is empty in the back seat. We drive home lighter than we left. Back at the shed the real week is not over, it is turning: trays that got cut this morning are already spoken for next Friday, and the empty racks want seeding again. But the delivery part, the part with the clock on it, is done by the time most people are on their second cup of coffee.

That is a Friday. A few hours from a dark shed to a porch across the county, run so the distance between when a green is cut and when you eat it stays as small as we can make it.
